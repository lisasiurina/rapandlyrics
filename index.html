Project Summary & Objectives

The Vision

In hip-hop culture, automobiles serve as more than just transportation; they are vital status symbols and cultural markers. The goal of this project was to quantitatively evaluate the presence of various car brands in song lyrics over a 35-year span (1980–2015).

Key Objectives

  • Extract a comprehensive list of global car brands via Wikipedia scraping.
  • Compile a massive lyric corpus from Billboard Rap Charts (4,800+ tracks).
  • Develop a search algorithm capable of handling slang and context.
  • Visualize popularity trends and brand evolution within the genre.

02. Brand Reference Preparation

Reference Extraction

Wikipedia lists of car manufacturers and brands by country were manually downloaded. To simplify the process and avoid complex HTML overhead, Mobile View was utilized for a cleaner DOM structure.

By running clean_carlist_wiki.py, brand name tags were extracted and cleaned using the Beautiful Soup library and NLTK for natural language refinement.

Disambiguation Logic

To maintain data integrity, certain car brands were explicitly excluded from the mining process. This prevents false positives caused by double meanings or unrelated proper names.

Excluded examples: Prince (artist name), MINI (common adjective), Oakland (city name).

Ensuring that "Prince" in lyrics referred to the brand, not the artist, was nearly impossible without advanced context, so such terms were pruned for accuracy.

03. Technical Pipeline

A

Lyrics Mining & Counting

Execution of reader1.py allowed for obtaining the total frequency of brand mentions per artist.

The script iterates through the compiled lyric archive, comparing tokens against the normalized reference list from hellacars.csv. 

# Core Logic Snippet
for artist in database:
    for brand in car_reference:
        # Case-insensitive substring matching
        count = raw_text.lower().count(brand.lower())
        stats[artist][brand] = count
B

Data Normalization (RegEx)

To handle the variety of slang used over 35 years, Regular Expressions were used to group identifiers. Mentions such as "Caddy", "DeVille", and "Fleetwood" were automatically aggregated under the primary **Cadillac** key.
